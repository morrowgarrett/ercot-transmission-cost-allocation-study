# Proxy Class Mapping

Proxy class mapping is an analytical overlay, not observed settlement truth.

The v1 framework uses proxy-class segmentation to compare how methodology burden *might* distribute between large-load and retail-oriented segments when direct customer-level evidence is unavailable in the public package.

## What v1 does
For each reporting unit, produce scenario overlays such as:
- conservative large-load share
- midpoint large-load share
- aggressive large-load share

Potential inputs may include:
- regional industrial concentration
- load-factor behavior
- public geography context
- known large-load corridor patterns
- any public-safe segmentation clues available for the reporting unit

## What v1 does not do
This framework does **not** claim that proxy-class outputs represent:
- actual settled customer classes
- exact industrial versus retail load splits
- legally operative billing segments
- customer-specific cost responsibility

## Why this matters
Proxy-class views are attractive because they make distributional questions legible.
They are also dangerous because they can look more precise than the evidence warrants.

A reviewer should assume:
- zonal outputs are primary
- proxy-class outputs are derived and lower-confidence
- sensitivity ranges matter more than any single midpoint split

## Required interpretation guardrails
When proxy-class results are shown, they should carry:
- explicit scenario labels
- confidence language
- a note that segmentation is modeled, not observed
- sensitivity coverage showing whether conclusions survive conservative and aggressive splits

## Directional risk
Proxy-class mapping can distort interpretation in several ways:
- flattening within-zone diversity
- overstating certainty about which customer segment benefits or loses
- smuggling normative assumptions into equity analysis
- making a methodology look more or less politically viable than the evidence really supports

## Reviewer question to ask
Do these proxy-class results reveal a robust directional pattern across segmentation scenarios, or are they just a story resting on one fragile split assumption?

That is the right question.
